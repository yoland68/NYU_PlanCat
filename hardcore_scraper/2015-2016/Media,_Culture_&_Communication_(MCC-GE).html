For the first time in history, computer-mediated culture has become mainstream for a majority of individuals in technologically developed societies. From e-mail to texting, from online gaming to online banking, from You Tube to Hulu, from DoS attacks to Second Life sit-ins, nearly every traditional aspect of our lives has found a new expression in its digital proxy. In order to understand the cultural, social, political and economic consequences of this development, we must look to the origins of today's cyberculture, in the futuristic visions (both dystonian and utopian) that shaped the development of today's networked technologies. We will trace the genealogy of these visions, as they developed in tandem with the growing digital communications infrastructure over the past three quarters of a century, and evolved into new forms that even the most forward-thinking of visionaries could not have predicted. Ultimately, one can argue the the Internet has both exceeded and fallen short of the hype that surrounded its birth and development, and by comparing the myth to the reality, we may better understand what aspects of the human condition are likely to persist regardless of technological development. less description for MCC-GE 2132

Notes: SAME AS CSCI-GA 3033013. Open to Doctoral Students Only. This course will be taught by Professor Natasha Schull. Topic: Self as Data. The past decade has seen a great deal of public discussion and debate over the ways that governments and corporations collect data on citizens and consumers, and the ends to which they use it. This seminar takes a different approach, focusing on cases in which individuals treat their own habits, bodies, moods, and thoughts as objects of scrutiny, analysis, and intervention. Ranging from 19th-century diary writing to contemporary diet techniques, Benjamin Franklins self-monitoring practices to the Quantified Self movements digital data collection apps, the course explores what different modes of self-examination, self-analysis, and self-governance might reveal about changing cultural values, political contexts, and understandings of the self, and how they remake subjectivity.
