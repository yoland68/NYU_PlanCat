ARTH-UA 3 Ancient Art

History of art in the Western tradition from 20,000 B.C. to the 4th century A.D. From the emergence of human beings in the Paleolithic Age to the developments of civilization in the Near East, Egypt, and the Aegean; the flowering of the Classical Age in Greece; and the rise of the Roman Empire to the beginnings of Christian domination under the Emperor Constantine in the 4th century A.D. Study of the collections of the Metropolitan Museum of Art and the Brooklyn Museum is essential.

ARTH-UA 4 Medieval Art

Art of Western civilization between Constantine and the Renaissance (300 to 1500 in northern Europe, 1400 in Italy). Topics: Christian beliefs underlying medieval art; acceptance and rejection of classical tradition and the roles of non-classical traditions in medieval art; stylistic transformations in medieval art in the context of medieval society; development of abbey and cathedral, monumental sculpture and painting, mosaics, stained glass, and fresco, as well as manuscript illumination, ivories, metalwork, and panel painting.

ARTH-UA 5 Renaissance Art

The Renaissance, like classical antiquity and the Middle Ages, is a major era of Western civilization embracing a multitude of styles. It is, however, held together by basic concepts that distinguish it from other periods. Main developments of Renaissance art both in Italy and north of the Alps: the Early and High Renaissance; relation to the lingering Gothic tradition; and Mannerism. Emphasis is placed on the great masters of each phase. The survival of Renaissance traditions in Baroque and Rococo art is examined in art and architecture.

The Art of the Egyptian Middle Kingdom Special Topics course, Fall 2015 Sandwiched between the impressive pyramids built by the Egyptians of the Old Kingdom and the glittering empire created by the pharaohs of the New Kingdom, Middle Kingdom Egypt (2055-1650 BCE) was a time of good government and political stability, which made possible great achievements in art, literature, and philosophy. This course will offer an in-depth look at the sculpture, painting, and architecture of the period, which looked back to the centuries-old traditions of the Pyramid Age while incorporating provincial innovations of the intervening intermediate period. Contemporary writings, including literary texts, autobiographies, and royal decrees, will illuminate developments in the content and style of the art works, illuminating the overall political, ideological, and religious programs of which they were a part. Timed to coincide with a major special exhibition in the art of the Middle Kingdom at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the course will feature several guest lectures, probably including some by curators involved with the exhibition.

Random sampling took off in the mid-late 20th century based on advances in the use of probability, statistics, and game theory applications to everyday life. After the publication by the RAND Corporation of a volume of one million random digits each number provably random the concept entered wide use. If artists of the early-mid 20th century had been concerned with chance, the notion of the random optimized it and brought it up to date for a new era. Computers have taken over the task of randomization, and it is their pseudo models that are now common. Contemporary artists today use the notion of random sampling in a more general sense, as we all do. The idea is pervasive. In art it can refer to the conceptual process, the selection of media, time and space, or an address to the audience. This seminar will explore the trajectory of these ideas from their highly systematic introduction last century to their omnipresence now.

This course is designed as an introduction to museum studies through the study of London museums. We will cover the types and definitions of museums, using key London collections, such as the British Museum and the Tate as well as smaller collections such as the Wallace Collection. The course will introduce contemporary theories and practices in museology, examine how collections evolve, interrogate the role of individual collectors, study the specific character of the permanent and temporary exhibitions, and discuss the relationship between museums, cultures, and society.
