The early modern period is frequently characterized as marking a moment of "epistemic rupture," a transition of dramatic proportions during which the grounds of what constituted certain knowledge changed. Several fundamental questions emerge as a consequence: how do we know something is true? When is it legitimate to question our beliefs about cherished values, morals, and assumptions about the order of the world? How does the intuition of radical uncertainty create new ethical dilemmas? And most central to our concerns: how do we describe the new literature that emerges to assuage the anxieties of doubt? This course traces and interrogates these questions by examining the intersections between Renaissance literature and the history of skepticism (broadly defined) - that is, the phenomenon of questioning our knowledge in many, if not all, domains in which we ordinarily think knowledge is possible. We will cast a wide net, ranging from cosmographies and travel accounts of New World discoveries, to the discovery of new planets and new scientific systems; from fictional utopias and imagined worlds to problems of philosophic truth and political action; and from Reformation and Counter-Reformation polemics to the new, hybrid interpretations of Genesis. less description for COLIT-UA 425 «

COLIT-UA 729 Traditional Drama China & Japan

The course will compare a set of Chinese and Japanese pre-modern dramas, mainly as literature but also as performance, by exploring the contrasts and parallels of incident, character, plot design, and theme of the two theatrical traditions. Attention will be given to the historical background of each work and to the social conditions and customs that each reflects. The cultural salience of each work is also considered. Where possible and appropriate scenes or entire plays will be screened for the class or assigned for viewing.
