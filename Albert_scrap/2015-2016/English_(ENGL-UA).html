Notes: In the 1960s and 70s, literary critics and theorists debated whether the author is “dead” and “what is an author” in the first place. ENGL-UA 181 Modernism and the City: London and New York

This course explores the cultural dynamics of transatlantic modernism as seen though the lens of urban experience. Focussing on London and New York as centers of gravity for modernist cultural, it looks at the reciprocal relationship between modernism and the city: how was modernism shaped by the urban experience, and how, in turn, did modernism help to mold our conception of the modern city? Notes: PREREQUISITES: ENGL-UA 220 Colloquium: Swift and Austen For two hundred years, critics have singled out for praise the prose style of Jonathan Swift and Jane Austen. Samuel Taylor Coleridge described Swift's style as "in its line, perfect; the manner is a complete expression of the matter, the terms appropriate, and the artifice concealed. It is simplicity in the true sense of the word." A century later, T. S. Eliot praised Swift as "the greatest writer of English prose, and the greatest man who has ever written great English prose." Jane Austen is even more widely known for her prose style: a combination of seemingly effortless elegance and acerbic wit. Yet nothing is "effortless" or straightforward in Austen, and Coleridge's praise of Swift's ability to "conceal" his "artifice" should suggest that this is also the case with Swift.
